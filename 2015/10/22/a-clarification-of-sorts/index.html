A Clarification Of Sorts...

22 October 2015 on Languages, Interests, Fun

In my last post, I stated that I didn't choose several languages to be the one that I focused on in my journey back into coding. While on the surface this is true, I must admit I have a hard time just "settling" on one language (or another (I have a similar problem with Linux distros, but that is a whole `nother story!)). There are so many fascinating nuances that different languages have, it is impossible for me to not pay at least some attention to other languages.

On that note, I would like to present the languages that I am going to be dabbling in, but not focusing on, and why I am interested in them (also, I am not happy with my "chosen language" post yet, but this one came out ok (it was originally intended to follow the "chosen one" post)).

First up is Python (https://www.python.org/), while it isn't ideal for my long-term goals, I still enjoy it a lot and it has a lot going for it in terms of community and continued evolution. However I will not be focusing on Python in its standard form, instead I will be using "Hy" to work with Python for the most part. Hy (http://docs.hylang.org/en/latest/index.html) is a LISP that generates a Python AST, and is fully compatible with Python (Yes, I'm a LISPaddict... just not a SmugLispWeenie (http://c2.com/cgi/wiki?SmugLispWeenie_)).

Next, is a language that I didn't mention in the previous post: TCL. TCL (http://tcl.tk/) is a small, light LISP-like (yep, again!) language, that is very cross-platform in the desktop arena, and great for lightweight GUI applications, and has the benefit of being LISP-like without a "variant" (like Python/Hy).

Alas, what good is a LISPaddict if they don't work with an "actual" LISP. While I truly do not have much (if any) interest in the "Common LISP" ecosystem, a small LISP named "Pixie" has caught my eye, and I'm following the project (https://github.com/pixie-lang/pixie) because it seems to be a very "modernized" approach to a LISP-esq language.

I did mention Racket in the previous post. Racket (http://racket-lang.org/) is a solid Scheme (http://tinyurl.com/9x9qkod (wikipedia)) implementation, and the IDE (DrRacket) has a lot of compatibility options for various Scheme standards. One of my favorite tutorials (How To Design Programs - HTDP) is written to take advantage of Racket's ability to "create" programming languages, to ease the introduction without forcing one to create "wimpy" or "useless" programs just to learn a language. For someone wanting to learn about making a program (vs. just coding one) it is my favorite recommendation. The website: http://www.schemers.org/ has many other implementations listed as well.

I also touched on "D" (http://dlang.org/). D claims (and feels to me) to be what C++ should be. It has seamless interop with C, and can be very low-level when needed, but has a very elegant high-level syntax for general use (it can even be used as a scripting language!). It strives to cherry-pick many of the best attributes of different programming paradigms (procedural, functional, and object-oriented), and let the coder flow between them at will.

As for the other mentioned languages (Haskell (https://www.haskell.org/), OCaml (http://ocaml.org/), Ruby (https://www.ruby-lang.org/), Common Lisp (https://common-lisp.net/), and Perl(https://www.perl.org/) (you can tell I'm a LISP-er because I'm ok nesting parenthesis!)), I will not be following these languages more than tangentially, just due to the limits of my brain, and time. This is not to say that they are inferior to what I have chosen (in many ways, they can be seen as superior), just that I do not feel that they "fit" me quite as well.

Well, now with that all out of the way, I better get working on what you have all been (patiently? I hope so!) waiting for: The language I've actually chosen!

Hope to be parsed by you again soon!

--The Perplexed One
