(= Clojure Awesome) => True

26 January 2016 on Clojure, Rant, Random

While digging into Clojure (admittedly I never stopped looking and marveling at it even while pursuing other languages), I have been unable to resist digging into what Lisps are. Partially in an attempt to understand them, and partially to understand why they work so well with me.

During these explorations I have come across an incredible amount of information that has helped me start to learn how to harness Clojure, and to apply it to the problems I intend to tackle.

Something that has always irked me during my multitude of searches and readings are the people (usually in the Common Lisp camp) who absolutely lose their shit when anyone dares claim anything but "Common Lisp" is a lisp at all. Some of them even go as far as to claim that Scheme is not a Lisp (it goes without saying that Clojure is most assuredly not a lisp in their eyes).

On the other side of the fence. Clojurians embrace the parts of lisp that have been incorporated into Clojure, and fully recognize that Clojure is not "Common Lisp" it is a language inspired by lisp, but forging its own path while not forgetting its roots.

The most superficial difference is probably the fact that Clojure uses all sorts of brackets, and not just parenthesis, this provides some visual distinction for different datatypes. While I don't have any problems with parenthesis as the overly-dominant form of bracket, I can also appreciate the visual clues provided by Clojure's syntax.

There are other differences due to Clojure being designed to run on the JVM (as well as JavaScript and CLR runtimes). The developers are doing a good job of not requiring one to use "native" (i.e. java) code in most cases, but just like the (not so well known) type annotations in Common Lisp (or the more well known ffi concepts), knowing what is going on under the hood does help, and will come in handy.

But wait, isn't there Common Lisp (and Scheme) on the JVM already? Yes, there is ABCL, and Kawa, the difference is those implementations attempt to preserve the syntax (easy) and semantics (not always as easy) of those languages, instead of taking concepts from those languages and implementing them in a way that is friendly with the JVM. This is the main reason I've chosen to go with Clojure first, it was designed from the ground up, to interop with the java world seamlessly. Common Lisp and Scheme are still somewhat islands unto themselves.

The "lonely island" phenomenon with Common Lisp and Scheme seems to have some root in the concept of "rolling your own" when it comes to implementing something that isn't provided by the language. It is true that both Common Lisp and Scheme offer the tools to do (just about?) anything you'd ever need to do with a language, as long as you are comfortable going very very deep into the language. It seems that doing something this way is almost (if not) a right of passage in those communities. While the achievement is definitely commendable, it also leads to a very fragmented and disjoint landscape for libraries that can even point someone in the right direction.

Clojure on the other hand, with its Java interop, as well as a constantly growing repository of quality native libraries, will almost always have something to get one moving in the right direction, regardless of what kind of project it is. On a quick side note, it is also nice that unlike with Java itself, Clojure tends to favor using a few small libraries to add functionality, than implement a large framework, it is a much more modular and (often) lightweight approach which I find quite elegant. These factors combined with the warm and welcoming community around Clojure are a major reason I kept drifting back towards it while digging in deep to language/application research these past couple months.

One of the neat things I've found in my research is that Clojure is "enough" of a lisp to be able to hand-implement many of the things Common Lisp can, but due to the incredible community, someone else already has, so if you are like me at this point, and want to do it yourself, you can find code (and often people!) who are willing to help you work through and truly understand what is going on.

While these are the main reasons I've been drawn (back) to Clojure, a couple others of note are: 1) It is standardized, there is just Clojure, not a bunch of different versions with slight differences in implementation details (most of which don't really matter until you get in "deep" but it is yet another level of fragmentation adding to the entry barrier). 2) It (Clojure) is (incredibly) easy to install and setup, and Leiningen is a tool very much in the spirit of Clojure itself (lightweight, simple, and works incredibly well). There are also online REPLs that make getting a "taste" very easy. And 3) Clojure is supported in all sorts of different editors, and usually supported quite well, ranging from the traditional emacs, to the slightly rebellious Vim, all the way up to more modern takes like Atom and LightTable, and full blown IDEs (Eclipse, IntelliJ). These factors make it a far more approachable language for those of us who need some help along the way.

--The Perplexed One
